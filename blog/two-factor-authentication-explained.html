Two-Factor Authentication Explained: TOTP, SMS, and Hardware Keys

September 2025 7 min read By Zecure Team

A strong password protects you from guessing and cracking. But if the site gets breached and your password hash leaks, you need a second line of defence. Not all two-factor methods are equal — here's what separates them.

Why a Password Alone Isn't Enough

Data breaches expose credentials constantly. Have I Been Pwned currently lists over 13 billion breached accounts. When a site is compromised, even a hashed password can be cracked if it's weak enough — and even a strong password becomes useless if you reuse it across sites.

Two-factor authentication (2FA) adds a second check: something you know (your password) plus something you have (a device or key). Even if an attacker gets your password, they can't log in without the second factor.

SMS 2FA: Better Than Nothing, But Not Much

SMS-based 2FA sends a one-time code to your phone number. It's the most widely deployed form of 2FA and the weakest in practice.

SIM swapping

An attacker calls your carrier, pretends to be you, and convinces them to transfer your phone number to a new SIM card. Your number now routes to their device. They receive your SMS codes. This attack has been used to drain cryptocurrency wallets, hijack social media accounts, and access email. It requires no hacking — just social engineering a carrier customer support rep.

SS7 interception

The Signaling System 7 protocol that underlies global phone infrastructure has known vulnerabilities that allow SMS messages to be intercepted by anyone with access to the SS7 network — including state-level actors and, in some regions, criminal groups.

NIST (SP 800-63B) deprecated SMS-based 2FA for government systems in 2017 due to these vulnerabilities. Use SMS 2FA only as a last resort — it's still better than no 2FA, but switch to TOTP or hardware keys when possible.

TOTP: The Right Default for Most People

Time-based One-Time Passwords (TOTP, defined in RFC 6238) are what apps like Google Authenticator, Authy, and 1Password generate. The six-digit code changes every 30 seconds.

How TOTP works

1
When you enable 2FA, the site generates a shared secret (a random 20-byte key) and shows it as a QR code.
2
Your authenticator app scans the QR code and stores the secret locally on your device.
3
Every 30 seconds, both your app and the server compute HMAC-SHA1(secret, floor(time / 30)) and derive a 6-digit code from the result.
4
When you log in, you enter the code. The server computes the same value and checks if they match. No network call required to generate the code.

TOTP doesn't depend on your phone number — it's immune to SIM swapping. But it's not fully phishing-proof: if you're tricked into typing your code into a fake login page, the attacker can relay it to the real site in real time (a "real-time phishing" attack).

Choosing a TOTP app

  • Ente Auth — open source, encrypted cloud backup, cross-platform. Best overall.
  • Aegis (Android) — open source, local encrypted backup, no cloud dependency.
  • 1Password / Bitwarden — integrates TOTP into your password manager. Convenient but combines "something you know" with 2FA.
  • Google Authenticator — no backup by default (now has Google account sync). Losing your phone meant losing all codes.
  • Authy — has cloud backup, but proprietary and has had security incidents.

Always save your backup codes when setting up TOTP. Store them in a password manager or printed in a secure location. Without them, losing your phone locks you out permanently if the site doesn't offer account recovery.

Hardware Keys: The Phishing-Proof Standard

Hardware security keys (YubiKey, Google Titan, Nitrokey) implement the FIDO2/WebAuthn standard. They're USB or NFC devices you physically touch to authenticate.

Why hardware keys can't be phished

FIDO2 uses public-key cryptography and binds authentication to the site's origin. When you register a key with example.com, the key creates a keypair specific to that exact domain. When you authenticate, it signs a challenge that includes the domain.

If a phishing site at examp1e.com tries to steal your login, your key refuses — the domain doesn't match. The attacker gets nothing usable. This is fundamentally different from TOTP, where the code is domain-agnostic and can be relayed.

Passkeys: hardware key logic without the hardware

Passkeys (the newer consumer-facing FIDO2 implementation) bring the same phishing-resistant cryptography to your phone or laptop. Your device generates a keypair per site; the private key never leaves the device. iCloud Keychain, Google Password Manager, and 1Password all support passkeys.

If a site offers passkeys, use them — they combine the security of hardware keys with the convenience of biometrics (Face ID, fingerprint) and cloud sync.

Which Should You Use?

  • For your most critical accounts (email, banking, password manager): hardware key or passkey. This is the tier where a breach costs the most.
  • For everything else: TOTP app. Set it up on every account that offers it. The improvement over SMS alone is substantial.
  • SMS 2FA: better than nothing. Enable it if it's the only option, but switch when better options become available.
  • Recovery codes: store them for every account, regardless of method. Loss of your device shouldn't mean permanent lockout.

Start with a strong password

2FA is your safety net — but a strong, unique password is still the first line of defense. Generate one instantly.
